\subsection{Comment 1: Distinction between TUSCO-novel and TUSCO-decoy}

\begin{reviewercomment}
The distinction between TUSCO-novel (annotation from those genes is removed) vs TUSCO-decoy (a misleading annotation is added in its place) should be made explicit, with results presented for both (along with the results when the full annotation is provided). It's debatable whether it's ``fair'' to provide inaccurate annotation. Minimap2 uses splice junction annotation for mapping so the alignments may already be biased before entering isoform discovery.
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
We apologize for the confusion---there is \textbf{no separate ``TUSCO-decoy'' approach}. What we previously referred to as a ``decoy'' is part of TUSCO-novel: it removes the true transcript from the annotation and replaces it with an artificial variant. To avoid ambiguity, we have removed the term ``decoy'' from the revised manuscript (abstract, line 67).

In TUSCO-novel, for each single-isoform gene in the TUSCO gene set, we generate an artificial transcript with modified splice junctions using canonical motifs (GT/AG) found in the genome sequence. This artificial transcript replaces the true annotation entirely. Tools must then reconstruct the correct (but now unannotated) isoform from the sequencing reads despite the presence of this misleading reference. See the Methods (lines 153--155) for technical details. We chose to replace rather than simply delete annotations because gene-level annotations are largely complete in human and mouse, while isoform-level annotations are often incomplete. This design mirrors a common real-world setting: the gene is known, but the splice pattern may be novel (e.g., tissue-, disease-, or condition-specific isoforms where annotations are unreliable). Simply deleting the annotation would not test this capability.

Fig.~4b presents results for both conditions in a dumbbell-plot format, directly comparing each metric under the correct reference annotation versus under TUSCO-novel. A quantitative summary is provided in Supplementary Table~2.

Regarding minimap2 splice junction bias, we share this concern. To prevent annotation bias from affecting our evaluation, \textit{we deliberately excluded all reference annotations during the minimap2 alignment stage}. Reads were aligned using only the genome sequence (FASTA), not gene models, ensuring alignments reflect genuine sequence evidence rather than annotation guidance. This design choice, while reducing initial alignment sensitivity compared to typical workflows, ensures that TUSCO-novel evaluates end-to-end, data-driven isoform discovery---the capability needed when working with incomplete or misleading annotations.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Changes in Manuscript:} We have updated the abstract to clarify the TUSCO-novel approach and removed the term ``decoy'' to avoid confusion:
\begin{quote}
``Masking transcripts in the TUSCO gene set and replacing them with modified splice variants in the annotation creates a TUSCO-novel challenge that assesses reconstruction of the true, now-unannotated isoforms.''
\end{quote}
\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Comment 4: Very deep datasets}

\begin{reviewercomment}
Would very deep datasets (100M reads?) cause issues, because rare transcription noise would still pass the threshold as a new isoform?
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
We agree that very deep sequencing (e.g., 100M reads) captures more rare events, but whether this ``noise'' is reported as novel isoforms depends heavily on the filtering strategies of the reconstruction tools:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{IsoQuant (Relative Thresholds):} IsoQuant filters novel transcripts that do not exceed 2\% of the maximum graph coverage. As sequencing depth increases, the absolute number of reads required to meet this 2\% threshold rises proportionally. Since stochastic transcription noise typically remains at low absolute frequencies, it fails to cross this dynamic threshold even in deep datasets.
    \item \textbf{Bambu (Probabilistic Modeling):} Bambu groups reads into ``Read Classes'' based on shared splice patterns and employs probabilistic modeling to identify high-confidence isoforms. Rare, stochastic noise that does not form consistent, high-confidence read classes is effectively filtered out during this consolidation process.
    \item \textbf{Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 (ML Filtering):} The SQANTI3 Machine Learning filter uses a Random Forest classifier trained on structural and quality attributes (e.g., TSS/TTS support, canonical junctions) to distinguish true isoforms from artifacts. This classifier learns from the features of annotated transcripts, which include expression levels, and it may call a rare transcript as true depending on how similar their features are to those of the annotated transcripts.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
If a tool fails to filter out this transcriptional noise and reports it as an isoform, TUSCO evaluation will identify it as a False Positive (FP), as genes in the TUSCO gene set are strictly single-isoform. Thus, TUSCO evaluation remains a valid benchmark for assessing a tool's ability to control for noise in deep datasets.
\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Comment 1: Statistical testing for SIRVs vs TUSCO}

\begin{reviewercomment}
Section 2.4 and Fig.~3b: When comparing the performance metrics for TUSCO and SIRVs, the authors mention that no significant differences were found for the TP and FN categories, whereas the figures suggest otherwise, i.e., SIRVs overtaking TUSCO for TPs. This could be because the statistical test was formulated for 'TUSCO $>$ SIRVs', for which the null hypothesis could not be rejected. The authors should incorporate testing for 'SIRVs $>$ TUSCO'.
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
Thank you for pointing this out. We revised this analysis to explicitly test both directions. Concretely, for Fig.~3b we now use a two-sided paired sign-flip permutation test on the paired pipeline-level differences ($n=6$ paired pipelines per species), which is exact for small $n$ and avoids distributional assumptions.

\vspace{0.3cm}
Following the logic of Fig.~3b, we first asked why one would compare the TUSCO gene set (endogenous controls) to spike-ins (SIRVs/Sequins): whether endogenous controls behave differently across pipelines/species than synthetic spike-ins. The primary observation is that spike-ins show higher TP rates than TUSCO evaluation (human TP $p=0.046$; mouse TP $p=0.015$). Decomposing where the ``missing'' TP goes shows it is driven by increases in PTP and FN rather than FP: PTP and FN are higher under TUSCO evaluation (human PTP $p=0.046$, FN $p=0.046$; mouse PTP $p=0.046$, FN $p=0.046$), while FP are not elevated (human FP $p=0.077$; mouse FP $p=0.877$). Intuitively, PTP indicates partial reconstruction (often truncation/incomplete capture) and FN indicates missed transcripts (often depth/protocol/RNA-quality limitations).

\vspace{0.3cm}
Our mechanistic interpretation is that, because transcripts in the TUSCO gene set are endogenous, they are exposed to RNA quality, extraction and library biases, and depth limitations that intact spike-ins do not experience. We test this directly in Fig.~3c: the fraction of fully reconstructed transcripts in the TUSCO gene set ($\frac{TP}{TP+PTP}$) correlates strongly with RIN, whereas Sequins does not. As secondary validation, we manually inspected representative false positives under TUSCO evaluation and found technical artifacts unsupported by long- and short-read evidence (Supplementary Notes~1--4), and the TUSCO gene set is defined from large short-read compendia as widely expressed, supporting the interpretation of FN as genuine non-recovery rather than lack of expression.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Changes in Manuscript:} We have rewritten the corresponding paragraph to reflect this logic and report two-sided paired test results:
\begin{quote}
``Across both species, spike-ins showed higher TP rates than \tuscoEvaluation{} (two-sided paired sign-flip permutation test across six pipelines, $n=6$ pairs; human TP $p=0.046$, mouse TP $p=0.015$). Decomposing this TP loss (still Fig.~3b) shows that it is driven by increases in PTP and FN rather than an explosion of FP: PTP and FN are higher under \tuscoEvaluation{} (human PTP $p=0.046$, FN $p=0.046$; mouse PTP $p=0.046$, FN $p=0.046$), whereas FP are not elevated (human FP $p=0.077$, mouse FP $p=0.877$). Intuitively, PTP reflects partial reconstruction (often truncation or incomplete capture), whereas FN reflects missed transcripts (often due to depth, protocol, or RNA quality limitations).''
\end{quote}
\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Comment 2: Reference annotations for novel transcript evaluation}

\begin{reviewercomment}
In evaluating the programs' (Bambu, StringTie2, FLAIR, Iso-Seq+SQANTI3 ML) performance for 'novel' transcripts, it is not clear what reference annotations were provided to the programs to create the radar plots. More specifically, did the annotations include all genes and variants in GENCODE, or only those related to the TUSCO gene set? Also, how were transcripts predicted by the programs at loci outside of the TUSCO gene set accounted for?
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
We appreciate the reviewer's request for clarification regarding the reference annotations used in the 'novel' transcript evaluation.

\textbf{1. Reference Annotations Provided:} For the 'novel' transcript benchmarking (Fig.~4), the reconstruction programs (Bambu, StringTie2, FLAIR, IsoQuant) were provided with a \textbf{comprehensive reference annotation} that included \textbf{all genes and isoforms in GENCODE}, with a specific modification restricted to the loci in the TUSCO gene set. For the genes in the TUSCO gene set, the true biological transcript models were removed and replaced by creating an entirely different splice pattern with artificial donor and acceptor sites at all junctions, supplying this artificially modified transcript as the sole reference. This modified full-genome annotation (e.g., \texttt{tusco\_human\_full.gtf}) served as the input guide for the genome-guided tools. For the Iso-Seq pipeline (official PacBio Iso-Seq pipeline + SQANTI3-ML filtering with complementary short-read sequencing data), this modified annotation was used during the SQANTI3 QC and Machine Learning filtering steps.

\textbf{2. Accounting for Transcripts Outside the TUSCO Gene Set:} Transcripts predicted by the programs at loci outside of the TUSCO gene set were \textbf{excluded from the performance metrics calculation}. As detailed in the ``Methods'' section under ``TUSCO and SIRV metrics calculation'', our evaluation was strictly restricted to reconstructed transcripts that mapped to genes in the TUSCO gene set, as these are our ``ground truth'' (identified by matching gene symbols or IDs in the SQANTI3 classification). Consequently, predictions at other genomic loci did not influence the sensitivity, precision, or other metrics shown in Fig.~4.
\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Major Gap 2: Comparison with short-read baseline approach}

\begin{reviewercomment}
In the abstract the authors postulate benchmarking has been done so far by two approaches: Spiked-in transcripts and synthetic data generation. The entire manuscript is focused on comparing to spiked-in transcripts to show the TUSCO benchmarking framework advantage. However, these are definitely not the only approaches. Specifically, recent work (MAJIQ-L, Han et al, Genome Research 2024) used short-reads sequenced from the same samples as a baseline to compare different long reads algorithms and long reads technology (ONT vs PacBio). While on the surface these are different evaluations (full isoforms reconstruction is not possible with short reads) in practice much of what the authors aim to assess (and what users care about) is totally achievable by analyzing short read sequences. Specifically, while transcript start/end are not available directly from short reads, Han et al pipeline allows users to assess where long reads report junctions, splice sites, and intron retention that do not exist by short reads (or annotation). Clearly, there could be advantages and disadvantages to assessing long reads with TUSCO evaluation vs. using short reads. For example, short reads require the matching experiment to be done with short reads and is not suitable for assessing start/end of transcripts. It also uses short-reads as the baseline which, by definition, cannot be considered an absolute truth but more of an approximation with its own biases. Given the importance of this to improving long reads transcriptomics benchmarking (the stated goal of this manuscript), the authors must investigate this thoroughly. Indeed Han et al used much of the same data from the long reads consortium and all their analysis/results are available. Thus, it should be relatively straightforward to compare TUSCO on the same data. The fact that Han et al used this orthogonal approach/method (MAJIQ based analysis) with the same data can help shed more light on TUSCO.

\subsection*{Metric Definitions}

To facilitate a fair comparison, we first define how benchmarking metrics are computed with each approach. For MAJIQ-L, junctions are classified as \textbf{known} (present in the annotation) or \textbf{novel} (not in the annotation):

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Known Junctions} & \textbf{Novel Junctions} \\
\midrule
Ground truth & All junctions identified by MAJIQ & All novel junctions identified by MAJIQ \\
\midrule
TP & TP-known: LR known + MAJIQ & TP-novel: LR novel + MAJIQ novel \\
FP & FP-known: LR known, no MAJIQ & FP-novel: LR novel, no MAJIQ \\
TN & \textit{Not possible (unknown expression)} & \textit{Not possible by MAJIQ} \\
FN & FN-known: No LR, Yes MAJIQ & FN-novel: No LR, Yes MAJIQ novel \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\vspace{0.3cm}

Note that \textbf{TN cannot be computed} with MAJIQ-L because we do not know if a gene is expressed.

\textbf{False Discovery Rate (FDR)} is computed as: FDR = $100 \times (N - TP) / N$, where N is the number of observed transcripts (TUSCO evaluation) or LR junctions (MAJIQ-L). For TUSCO evaluation, N = TP + PTP + FP (observed transcripts); for MAJIQ-L, N = total\_LR\_junctions and TP includes both known and novel.

\subsection*{Comparison of Benchmarking Insights}

Supplementary Fig.~4 compares Sensitivity, FN rate, and FDR between TUSCO evaluation and MAJIQ-L across all pipelines. Only cDNA-PacBio consistently ranks best in both methods. The rankings for cDNA-ONT and dRNA-ONT differ substantially between TUSCO evaluation and MAJIQ-L, revealing that each method captures different aspects of data quality.

TUSCO evaluation is sensitive to sample quality, particularly RNA integrity. The cDNA-ONT pipeline shows 38.3\% PTP transcripts, indicating incomplete RNA molecules with poor TSS/TTS accuracy. This results in low sensitivity (59.5\%) and high FDR (51.1\%). In contrast, dRNA-ONT achieves high sensitivity (75.6\%) despite having only 760K reads (compared to 16.6M for cDNA-ONT), suggesting that the RNA molecules in this preparation are more intact.

MAJIQ-L, on the other hand, reflects sequencing depth because it evaluates all splice junctions rather than focusing on consistently expressed genes like those in the TUSCO gene set. The dRNA-ONT pipeline has only 760K reads (22-fold fewer than cDNA-ONT's 16.6M reads) and consequently misses many lowly-expressed junctions, leading to a high FN rate (73.5\%) in MAJIQ-L. Importantly, the low FDR in dRNA-ONT (2.0\% by MAJIQ-L, 8.1\% by TUSCO evaluation) confirms that sample quality is good---the high FN rate is due to depth limitations, not poor sample quality. The cDNA-ONT pipeline has 16.6M reads, detecting more junctions and achieving higher MAJIQ-L sensitivity (37.0\%) compared to dRNA-ONT (26.5\%).

These complementary patterns demonstrate that TUSCO evaluation and MAJIQ-L assess fundamentally different quality dimensions.

The two methods differ fundamentally in their approach. MAJIQ-L evaluates junction-level metrics and can assess novel junctions not present in the annotation, while TUSCO evaluation evaluates transcript-level metrics including full transcript structure and is limited to consistently expressed genes in the TUSCO gene set. A key advantage of TUSCO evaluation is its ability to evaluate TSS and TTS accuracy through the PTP (partially true positive) category, which MAJIQ-L cannot assess. Furthermore, TUSCO evaluation provides a true ground truth based on the reference annotation, whereas MAJIQ-L uses short-read data as an approximation of the truth.

\subsection*{Conclusion}

TUSCO evaluation and MAJIQ-L are complementary benchmarking approaches that capture different aspects of long-read transcriptomics performance. TUSCO evaluation provides a true annotation-based ground truth rather than relying on short-read approximations, can evaluate both PTP and FN at the transcript level, requires no additional sequencing costs (no matched short-read data needed), and uniquely captures TSS and TTS accuracy through the PTP category. We acknowledge that the TUSCO gene set is not defined for novel species and has not been tested in disease settings.

\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Additional Improvement 2: ``Biologically plausible'' definition}

\begin{reviewercomment}
The TUSCO-novel claim of ``biologically plausible isoforms'' (section 2.5) is interesting, but the validity of ``biologically plausible'' is unclear. Were the made-up splice sites chosen to e.g. preserve splice motifs / enhancers, etc? What about exon length and GC content bias?
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
In the current \tuscoNovel{} simulator implementation, the artificial isoforms are designed to be \emph{structurally plausible} in the limited sense of satisfying basic splice-junction sequence constraints. For multi-exon transcripts, each annotated intron is modified by shifting the donor and acceptor coordinates \emph{within the original intron bounds} while searching the gene's genomic sequence for canonical dinucleotides: \texttt{GT} at the donor and \texttt{AG} at the acceptor on the plus strand, and the corresponding reference-strand motifs \texttt{AC} (donor) and \texttt{CT} (acceptor) on the minus strand. The search is directional (donor downstream / acceptor upstream on the plus strand) with a default window of 100~bp, falling back to scanning the full intron if needed, and it enforces a minimum shift of 5~bp when a suitable motif can be found. For single-exon transcripts, the simulator introduces one synthetic intron by selecting two positions separated by at least 50~bp and then adjusting each boundary to a nearby canonical motif within 20~bp when possible.

That said, several aspects of \emph{biological} plausibility are not modeled or guaranteed. Splice enhancer/silencer sequences are not preserved, branch point / polypyrimidine tract features are not verified, and GC content or exon-length distributions are not matched. The goal is not to generate transcripts that would necessarily be spliced in vivo, but to create misleading annotations that are superficially consistent with simple splice-site rules and could plausibly appear in a reference annotation. This tests whether reconstruction tools primarily follow the supplied annotation or instead use read evidence to recover the true splice pattern.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Changes in Manuscript:} We have revised the term to ``structurally plausible'' rather than ``biologically plausible'' and clarified in the Methods that artificial splice sites preserve canonical motifs but do not guarantee functional splicing.
\end{responseboxnew}

Cosine similarity measures the angular similarity between two 6-dimensional metric vectors (Sn, nrPre, Redundancy$^{-1}$, 1-FDR, PDR, rPre). Values above 0.99 suggest that TUSCO evaluation and SIRVs report virtually identical relative performance, indicating both benchmarks assess the same underlying pipeline quality. \subsection{Additional Improvement 6: Per-read coverage definition}

\begin{reviewercomment}
It is not clear why per-read coverage is defined and used as in Section 2.5.
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
\textbf{Why we define and use per-read coverage here:} In Section~2.5, we ask why some \tuscoGeneSet{} loci are TP (full match, including boundaries) while others are PTP (same junction chain, but shifted TSS/TTS). For Iso-Seq, many PTPs are expected from 5'/3' truncated molecules (e.g., RNA degradation or incomplete capture): reads can support the correct junction chain but fail to span the true transcript ends, yielding boundary-shifted models unless transcript ends are refined. Per-read coverage quantifies this end-completeness as a read-length ratio relative to the reference isoform.

\textbf{Definition:} For each supporting read, we compute
$f = L_{\mathrm{read}}/L_{\mathrm{exonic}}$, where $L_{\mathrm{exonic}}$ is the total exonic length of the \emph{true reference isoform}, and $L_{\mathrm{read}}$ is the observed Iso-Seq read length (PacBio cDNA; FLNC). With this definition, $f \approx 1$ indicates a near full-length molecule, $f \ll 1$ indicates truncation, and $f$ can exceed 1 when a read is longer than the reference isoform (e.g., extra UTR/poly(A) sequence or boundary differences).

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{TP vs.\ PTP boundary effects:} If PTP is driven by truncation, PTP-supporting reads should have lower $f$ than TP-supporting reads. Consistent with this, TP reads show high coverage (median 98.3\% in human), whereas PTP reads show substantially lower coverage (median 31.7\% in human).

    \item \textbf{Interpretation in our PacBio cDNA data:} Lower $f$ indicates more truncated/end-incomplete molecules (consistent with degradation and/or incomplete capture), which explains boundary-shifted transcript models when TSS/TTS are not refined.
\end{enumerate}

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Changes in Manuscript:} We have added explicit justification for this metric definition in Section 2.5 and clarified its interpretation in the figure caption.
\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Comment 2: Section 2.5 - TUSCO-novel evaluation}

\begin{reviewercomment}
The capacity of reconstruction tools to detect unannotated isoforms is assessed with used of introduced TUSCO-novel reference set. The authors note that tools which heavily rely on annotation fail to recover new isoforms as defined in TUSCO-novel. That is true, but what was not highlighted or noted is the fact that preferred by authors Iso-Seq+SQANTI3 is not way better. It has marginal drop of performance between ``reference annotation'' and TUSCO-novel due to the fact that is dealing with ``reference annotation'' noticeably worse than other approaches. Thus ability of TUSCO-novel to evaluate novel isoforms discovery is not that remarkable I would say.
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
The reviewer makes an astute observation. Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 ML indeed shows lower absolute performance with the reference annotation than strongly annotation-guided tools, and its minimal change under TUSCO-novel reflects this lower baseline rather than superior novel discovery. For example, summarizing Fig.~4b by a composite score defined as the mean of the six \tuscoMetrics{} (Sn, nrPre, $\mathrm{Redundancy}^{-1}$, $1-\mathrm{FDR}$, PDR, rPre), the median reference score is 76.29 (human) for Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 ML versus 100.00 for Bambu, while the median TUSCO-novel change for Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 ML is near zero ($-0.73\%$ in human; Supplementary Table~2).

The key insight from TUSCO-novel is not absolute performance but annotation dependence. Bambu and StringTie2 are strongly reference-guided and therefore highly dependent on reference annotation; in settings where gene-level annotation is largely complete but isoform-level annotation is missing or incorrect, this reliance can limit their ability to detect genuinely novel isoforms. Consistent with this, when the annotation at loci in the TUSCO gene set is replaced by an incorrect splice pattern, Bambu and StringTie2 fail substantially to recover the true isoforms despite read support. Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 ML, by contrast, performs consistently regardless of annotation status. The lower baseline for Iso-Seq arises because the pipeline lacks TSS/TTS refinement against the reference, resulting in more PTP calls even when the junction chain is correct. This reduces TP rates but makes the pipeline inherently reference-agnostic.

The practical implication is that in scenarios where reference annotations may be incomplete or misleading---such as novel tissues, disease states, or non-model organisms---a pipeline with lower but stable performance may be preferable to one with high reference-guided performance that collapses without correct annotation.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Changes in Manuscript:} We have revised Section 2.5 to explicitly acknowledge this trade-off:

\begin{quote}
``Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 ML showed minimal performance change under TUSCO-novel ($-1.9\%$ to $+2.7\%$), though with lower absolute performance than annotation-guided tools. This stability arises from the pipeline's reference-agnostic design, which maintains consistent (if moderate) performance regardless of annotation quality---a potentially valuable property when working with incomplete or unreliable references.''
\end{quote}
\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Comment 3: Section 2.6 - Replicate number recommendations}

\begin{reviewercomment}
It is stated: ``... an optimal design includes at least three biological replicates (Fig.~5b; Supplementary Table~3). By contrast, analyses of SIRV spike-in controls yielded highly consistent concordance metrics across 1–5 replicates, providing little guidance on replicate number optimization (Supplementary Fig.~6).''

\vspace{0.2cm}
An inspection of Fig.~5 together with Supplementary Fig.~6 reveals that there are no drastic differences across 2-5 replicates for both approaches. Thus statement that for TUSCO a good choice is 3+ while for SIRV it is hard to say is overinterpretation. Also here I do not understand what Supplementary Table~3 is adding here.
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
We agree that the differences between 2--5 replicates are modest for both TUSCO evaluation and SIRVs, and our original phrasing may have overstated the guidance provided.

The most substantial improvement occurs between 1 and 2 replicates, with FDR dropping from 32.9\% to 12.2\% in brain and from 34.8\% to 19.0\% in kidney. Beyond 2 replicates, gains are incremental. The key difference between the two benchmarks is that TUSCO metrics do change with replicate number (albeit modestly after 2 replicates), while SIRVs are essentially invariant. This demonstrates that TUSCO evaluation captures real experimental variation that SIRVs miss, even if the absolute differences are small. Regarding Supplementary Table~3, the per-sample FLNC read counts provide the depth context needed to interpret these replicate effects and to generalize them: they allow readers to compare their expected per-sample yield to ours and, under a fixed sequencing budget, to evaluate the trade-off between sequencing deeper versus adding replicates.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Changes in Manuscript:} We have revised Section 2.6 to present findings more objectively:

\begin{quote}
``Collectively, these results indicate that the \tuscoEvaluation{} effectively reveals the effect of replication on transcript detection accuracy. The largest precision gain occurred between one and two replicates, with additional replicates (3--5) yielding incremental improvements at this sequencing depth (Supplementary Table~3). Thus, for this experimental setup (mouse brain/kidney, PacBio cDNA, FLAIR), at least two biological replicates substantially improve precision, with diminishing returns thereafter; additional replicates can still provide modest sensitivity gains (notably in kidney) via consensus boundary refinement (Fig.~5b). By contrast, analyses of SIRV spike-in controls yielded highly consistent concordance metrics across 1--5 replicates, providing little guidance on replicate number optimization (Supplementary Fig.~7).''
\end{quote}
\end{responseboxnew}
