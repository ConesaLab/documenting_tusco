TUSCO gene selection pipeline
TUSCO was constructed with a single, reproducible pipeline that integrates multi-source annotations, population splicing and TSS evidence, and broad expression compendia, and then enforces strict “no evidence for alternative isoforms” criteria. For human, we retrieved and harmonized GENCODE43 v49, RefSeq44 GRCh38.p14 (GCF_000001405.40), and MANE45 v1.4 annotations together with an Ensembl BioMart Ensembl↔RefSeq/NCBI mapping46; or mouse, we used GENCODE vM38 and RefSeq GRCm39 with the corresponding Ensembl BioMart mapping. After normalizing chromosome labels where needed, we identified genes that are single-isoform in each source and required exact identity of exon–intron structure and strand across all GTFs; only those cross-annotation matches were retained and propagated with synchronized mapping subsets.
We next screened for population-level alternative splicing and TSS inconsistencies. Splice evidence was derived from recount3 junction BEDs28. For each multi-exon gene, we computed the mean coverage of annotated junctions, \mu, and declared a novel junction as supporting an alternative isoform if it was fully intragenic, strand-consistent, and its coverage exceeded T = 0.05 * \mu, with a minimum absolute cutoff of one supporting read; novel junctions shorter than 80 bp were ignored. TSS evidence was assessed with refTSS v4.133. In human, TSS screening was restricted to single-exon loci: we required both an exon-overlap check (exactly one refTSS interval must overlap the TSS exon and contain the transcript’s TSS coordinate) and a CAGE window check centered at the transcript TSS that removed loci with more than one peak in a ±300 bp window; multi-exon loci were not evaluated with a TSS window. In mouse, TSS screening was restricted to single-exon genes using the exon-overlap rule. For human only, we further removed genes flagged by IntroVerse_80 as having widespread novel isoform usage in population data (>80% of samples)32.
Expression-based universality and tissue sets were then established using the resources and thresholds specified in the analysis configuration. For human, we applied a hybrid approach: Bgee present/absent calls and RNA-Seq library metadata (accepting “gold quality”, “silver quality”, and “bronze quality”) were used to define universal genes, restricting analysis to tissues with at least 25,000 distinct expressed genes; genes were called universal if present in at least 95% of retained tissues34. Tissue-specific gene sets were derived from GTEx using median TPM ≥ 1.0 and prevalence ≥ 95% at TPM > 0.1 across samples for a tissue. For mouse, we used Bgee with the same minimum tissue gene coverage (25,000) and accepted “gold quality” and “silver quality” calls, calling universal genes at a 90% prevalence threshold across retained tissues. Anatomical entity IDs were mapped to tissue names, and per-tissue gene sets were emitted using those IDs. To reinforce universality, we merged housekeeping genes from HRT Atlas into the universal set when available35. Where specified (e.g., mouse kidney), we intersected tissue-specific sets with ENCODE-derived calls using median TPM ≥ 1.0 and prevalence ≥ 95% at TPM > 0.1 [ENCODE].
To ensure consistency at the population level beyond presence/absence, we integrated an AlphaGenome-based RPKM screen into the expression step with species- and class-specific thresholds37. Universal genes were retained only if they satisfied both the expression-source criteria above and the AlphaGenome criteria. In human, genes were retained only if the median RPKM across mapped tissues was ≥ 1.0 for both single-exon and multi-exon classes; if at least 95% of tissues showed RPKM exceeding 0.1; and if at least 95% of tissues had an unannotated-to-annotated splice ratio < 0.01 (splice purity ≥ 0.95). In mouse, the corresponding criteria were: median RPKM ≥ 0.5 (single-exon) or ≥ 0.25 (multi-exon); prevalence ≥ 90% at RPKM > 0.01 (single-exon) or > 0.005 (multi-exon); and splice purity ≥ 0.90 with splice ratio < 0.01. When reporting tissue-specific sets, an additional tissue-level AlphaGenome screen was applied using tissue RPKM and splice-ratio thresholds; for human we required RPKM > 4.0 and splice ratio < 0.001 in the tissue of interest, and for mouse RPKM > 1.5 and splice ratio < 0.001. A small, curated exclusion list was applied at the end to remove remaining edge cases. The pipeline writes synchronized mapping subsets alongside each GTF subset and records, for every removed gene, the first filter responsible for the removal.
