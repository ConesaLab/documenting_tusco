\subsection{Comment 1: Distinction between TUSCO-novel and TUSCO-decoy}

\begin{reviewercomment}
The distinction between TUSCO-novel (annotation from those genes is removed) vs TUSCO-decoy (a misleading annotation is added in its place) should be made explicit, with results presented for both (along with the results when the full annotation is provided). It's debatable whether it's ``fair'' to provide inaccurate annotation. Minimap2 uses splice junction annotation for mapping so the alignments may already be biased before entering isoform discovery.
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
We apologize for the confusion---there is \textbf{no separate ``TUSCO-decoy'' approach}. What we previously referred to as a ``decoy'' is part of TUSCO-novel: it removes the true transcript from the annotation and replaces it with an artificial variant. To avoid ambiguity, we have removed the term ``decoy'' from the revised manuscript (abstract, lines 29--31).

In TUSCO-novel, for each single-isoform gene in the TUSCO gene set, we generate an artificial transcript with modified splice junctions using canonical motifs (GT/AG) found in the genome sequence. This artificial transcript replaces the true annotation entirely. Tools must then reconstruct the correct (but now unannotated) isoform from the sequencing reads despite the presence of this misleading reference. See the Methods (lines 623--638) for technical details. We chose to replace rather than simply delete annotations because gene-level annotations are largely complete in human and mouse, while isoform-level annotations are often incomplete. This design mirrors a common real-world setting: the gene is known, but the splice pattern may be novel (e.g., tissue-, disease-, or condition-specific isoforms where annotations are unreliable). Simply deleting the annotation would not test this capability.

Fig.~4b presents results for both conditions in a dumbbell-plot format, directly comparing each metric under the correct reference annotation versus under TUSCO-novel. A quantitative summary is provided in Supplementary Table~3.

Regarding minimap2 splice junction bias, we share this concern. To prevent annotation bias from affecting our evaluation, \textit{we deliberately excluded all reference annotations during the minimap2 alignment stage}. Reads were aligned using only the genome sequence (FASTA), not gene models, ensuring alignments reflect genuine sequence evidence rather than annotation guidance. This design choice, while reducing initial alignment sensitivity compared to typical workflows, ensures that TUSCO-novel evaluates end-to-end, data-driven isoform discovery---the capability needed when working with incomplete or misleading annotations.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Changes in Manuscript:} We have updated the abstract to clarify the TUSCO-novel approach and removed the term ``decoy'' to avoid confusion:
\begin{quote}
``Masking transcripts in the TUSCO gene set and replacing them with modified splice variants in the annotation creates a TUSCO-novel challenge that assesses reconstruction of the true, now-unannotated isoforms.''
\end{quote}
\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Comment 4: Very deep datasets}

\begin{reviewercomment}
Would very deep datasets (100M reads?) cause issues, because rare transcription noise would still pass the threshold as a new isoform?
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
We agree that very deep sequencing (e.g., 100M reads) captures more rare events, but whether this ``noise'' is reported as novel isoforms depends heavily on the filtering strategies of the reconstruction tools:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{IsoQuant (Relative Thresholds):} IsoQuant filters novel transcripts that do not exceed 2\% of the maximum graph coverage. As sequencing depth increases, the absolute number of reads required to meet this 2\% threshold rises proportionally. Since stochastic transcription noise typically remains at low absolute frequencies, it fails to cross this dynamic threshold even in deep datasets.
    \item \textbf{Bambu (Probabilistic Modeling):} Bambu groups reads into ``Read Classes'' based on shared splice patterns and employs probabilistic modeling to identify high-confidence isoforms. Rare, stochastic noise that does not form consistent, high-confidence read classes is effectively filtered out during this consolidation process.
    \item \textbf{Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 (ML Filtering):} The SQANTI3 Machine Learning filter uses a Random Forest classifier trained on structural and quality attributes (e.g., TSS/TTS support, canonical junctions) to distinguish true isoforms from artifacts. This classifier learns from the features of annotated transcripts, which include expression levels, and it may call a rare transcript as true depending on how similar their features are to those of the annotated transcripts.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.3cm}
If a tool fails to filter out this transcriptional noise and reports it as an isoform, TUSCO evaluation will identify it as a False Positive (FP), as genes in the TUSCO gene set are strictly single-isoform. Thus, TUSCO evaluation remains a valid benchmark for assessing a tool's ability to control for noise in deep datasets.
\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Comment 1: Statistical testing for SIRVs vs TUSCO}

\begin{reviewercomment}
Section 2.4 and Fig.~3b: When comparing the performance metrics for TUSCO and SIRVs, the authors mention that no significant differences were found for the TP and FN categories, whereas the figures suggest otherwise, i.e., SIRVs overtaking TUSCO for TPs. This could be because the statistical test was formulated for 'TUSCO $>$ SIRVs', for which the null hypothesis could not be rejected. The authors should incorporate testing for 'SIRVs $>$ TUSCO'.
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
Thank you for pointing this out. We revised this analysis to explicitly test both directions using a two-sided paired sign-flip permutation test.

\vspace{0.3cm}
This result is expected and informative. TUSCO evaluation shows significantly higher rates of Partial True Positives (PTP) and False Negatives (FN) compared to SIRVs (two-sided paired sign-flip permutation test, $n=6$ paired pipelines per species; human PTP $p=0.046$, FN $p=0.046$; mouse PTP $p=0.046$, FN $p=0.046$). The corresponding lower TP rate (human $p=0.046$; mouse $p=0.015$) is driven by these increases in PTP and FN rather than an explosion of FP, which are not significantly elevated (human FP $p=0.077$; mouse FP $p=0.877$).

\vspace{0.3cm}
This difference arises because genes in the \tuscoGeneSet{} are endogenous and thus subject to biological processes like RNA degradation (leading to PTP) and protocol-specific limitations (leading to FN). In contrast, synthetic SIRV spike-ins are added at high concentrations and are not subject to extraction-induced degradation. We have demonstrated these effects in our RNA degradation analysis (Fig.~3c), where the fraction of fully reconstructed transcripts in the TUSCO gene set ($\frac{TP}{TP+PTP}$) correlates strongly with RIN (Pearson $R=0.810$, $p=4.60 \times 10^{-5}$), whereas Sequins showed no correlation ($R=0.075$, $p=0.77$). FN rates are further shown to be dominated by platform/protocol choice in Supplementary Fig.~3. Therefore, the lower TP rate in TUSCO evaluation accurately reflects the true sample quality and protocol limitations, providing a more realistic benchmark than the artificially inflated performance seen with SIRVs.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Changes in Manuscript:} We have updated Section 2.4 (lines 221--229) to explicitly report this significant difference using two-sided tests and explain that it reflects the sensitivity of TUSCO evaluation to sample quality factors that SIRVs do not capture, citing our specific analyses as evidence:
\begin{quote}
``Across both species, spike-ins showed higher TP rates than \tuscoEvaluation{} (two-sided paired sign-flip permutation test across six pipelines, $n=6$ pairs; human TP $p=0.046$, mouse TP $p=0.015$). Closer inspection of the lower TP for \tuscoEvaluation{} (Fig.~3b) shows that it is driven by increases in PTP and FN rather than an explosion of FP: PTP and FN are higher under \tuscoEvaluation{} (human PTP $p=0.046$, FN $p=0.046$; mouse PTP $p=0.046$, FN $p=0.046$), whereas FP are not elevated (human FP $p=0.077$, mouse FP $p=0.877$). We hypothesized that PTP arises from RNA degradation causing truncated molecules, while FN reflects protocol limitations; we tested these relationships directly.''
\end{quote}
\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Comment 2: Reference annotations for novel transcript evaluation}

\begin{reviewercomment}
In evaluating the programs' (Bambu, StringTie2, FLAIR, Iso-Seq+SQANTI3 ML) performance for 'novel' transcripts, it is not clear what reference annotations were provided to the programs to create the radar plots. More specifically, did the annotations include all genes and variants in GENCODE, or only those related to the TUSCO gene set? Also, how were transcripts predicted by the programs at loci outside of the TUSCO gene set accounted for?
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
We appreciate the reviewer's request for clarification regarding the reference annotations used in the 'novel' transcript evaluation. For the benchmarking in Fig.~4, the reconstruction programs (Bambu, StringTie2, FLAIR, IsoQuant) were provided with a comprehensive reference annotation that included all genes and isoforms in GENCODE, with a specific modification restricted to the loci in the TUSCO gene set. At these loci, the true biological transcript models were removed and replaced by artificial variants with entirely different splice patterns, using canonical donor and acceptor sites at all junctions. This modified full-genome annotation (e.g., \texttt{gencode\_tusco\_novel\_hsa.gtf}) served as the input guide for the genome-guided tools. For the Iso-Seq pipeline (official PacBio Iso-Seq pipeline + SQANTI3-ML filtering with complementary short-read sequencing data), this modified annotation was used during the SQANTI3 QC and Machine Learning filtering steps.

Transcripts predicted by the programs at loci outside of the TUSCO gene set were excluded from the performance metrics calculation. As detailed in the ``Methods'' section under ``TUSCO and SIRV metrics calculation'', our evaluation was strictly restricted to reconstructed transcripts that mapped to genes in the TUSCO gene set, as these constitute our ground truth (identified by matching gene symbols or IDs in the SQANTI3 classification). Consequently, predictions at other genomic loci did not influence the sensitivity, precision, or other metrics shown in Fig.~4.
\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Comment 2: Comparison with short-read baseline approach}

\begin{reviewercomment}
In the abstract the authors postulate benchmarking has been done so far by two approaches: Spiked-in transcripts and synthetic data generation. The entire manuscript is focused on comparing to spiked-in transcripts to show the TUSCO benchmarking framework advantage. However, these are definitely not the only approaches. Specifically, recent work (MAJIQ-L, Han et al, Genome Research 2024) used short-reads sequenced from the same samples as a baseline to compare different long reads algorithms and long reads technology (ONT vs PacBio). While on the surface these are different evaluations (full isoforms reconstruction is not possible with short reads) in practice much of what the authors aim to assess (and what users care about) is totally achievable by analyzing short read sequences. Specifically, while transcript start/end are not available directly from short reads, Han et al pipeline allows users to assess where long reads report junctions, splice sites, and intron retention that do not exist by short reads (or annotation). Clearly, there could be advantages and disadvantages to assessing long reads with TUSCO evaluation vs. using short reads. For example, short reads require the matching experiment to be done with short reads and is not suitable for assessing start/end of transcripts. It also uses short-reads as the baseline which, by definition, cannot be considered an absolute truth but more of an approximation with its own biases. Nonetheless, using short read carry important potential benefits: It can be done for any condition easily and will not be sensitive to the definition of synthetically introduced transcripts in the TUSCO gene set or to actual splice variants occurring in genes in the TUSCO gene set in a condition/disease. It also gives a much broader ``natural'' set of genes with all their built in complexity without resorting to special case genes which may be biased in some unknown way (more on that later). It also gives a wider spread of TPM, gene length etc. to assess performance, important factors glossed over in the current manuscript. Consequently, it may offer much more accurate assessment of false negatives (see below).

While the above qualitative assessment may be sensible it is also speculative and not quantitative. Given the importance of this to improving long reads transcriptomics benchmarking (the stated goal of this manuscript), the authors must investigate this thoroughly. Indeed Han et al used much of the same data from the long reads consortium and all their analysis/results are available. Thus, it should be relatively straightforward to compare TUSCO on the same data. The fact that Han et al used this orthogonal approach/method (MAJIQ based analysis) with the same data can help shed more light on TUSCO.

\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}

We thank the reviewer for this suggestion.

    \vspace{0.3cm}
 We agree that short reads have historically been used to support or validate long-read transcript models. For instance, FLAIR incorporates short-read evidence to identify novel-not-in-catalogue transcripts, and SQANTI3 employs short-read support as a key QC feature. Short-read--based benchmarking can indeed increase the number of transcript models available for evaluation, although this expansion is inherently limited to transcripts containing unique junctions that short reads can confidently resolve.
At the same time, short-read benchmarking introduces well-recognized limitations. Because short reads lack contiguity, they cannot directly validate transcript-level structures, leaving open the question of whether long-read--derived transcript models---especially novel or recombined ones---are correctly reconstructed end-to-end. As the reviewer notes, short reads also cannot reliably assess TSS or TTS, which remain among the most challenging features of long-read RNA-seq to benchmark. Looking forward, as long-read sequencing technologies continue to increase in throughput and decrease in cost, matched short-read datasets may become less common, reducing the practicality of relying on short reads for benchmarking in many experimental settings.

    \vspace{0.3cm}
    As a consequence, \textbf{\tuscoEvaluation{}} and \textbf{MAJIQ-L} possess fundamentally different scopes and target distinct dimensions of transcriptome reconstruction quality: \textbf{MAJIQ-L} focuses on \textit{junction-level} comparison, identifying where long reads report junctions, splice sites, and intron
        retention that differ from short-read evidence. This is excellent for detecting junction-level errors but uses short reads as an approximation of truth rather than an absolute ground truth. In contrast, \textbf{\tuscoEvaluation{}} focuses on \textit{full-length transcript reconstruction}, including TSS/TTS boundaries that short reads cannot assess. \tuscoEvaluation{} provides an absolute ground truth (single-isoform genes with no evidence of alternative splicing) rather than a relative baseline. Therefore a direct comparison is not straightforward.
    Nevertheless, we agree with the reviewer that a quantitative comparison against the short-read--based approach of Han et al. (MAJIQ-L, Genome Research 2024) is interesting, particularly because their study used much of the same LRGASP material. We have therefore performed this analysis. Specifically, we re-processed the datasets using the same versions of the tools evaluated in our study to ensure methodological consistency. We then compared \tuscoMetrics{} with those derived from the MAJIQ-based short-read benchmarking approach across all tools included in our manuscript.

The results of this comparative analysis are now included in the revised version of the manuscript. We have performed a systematic comparison of the benchmarking metrics that can be computed with TUSCO evaluation versus MAJIQ-L, and present the results below (Supplementary Fig.~4).

\subsection*{Metric Definitions}

To facilitate a fair comparison, we first define how benchmarking metrics are computed with each approach in a manner comparable to the \tuscoBenchmarkingFramework{}'s definitions. For MAJIQ-L, junctions are classified as \textbf{known} (present in the annotation) or \textbf{novel} (not in the annotation):

\vspace{0.3cm}
\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
\textbf{Metric} & \textbf{Known Junctions} & \textbf{Novel Junctions} \\
\midrule
Ground truth & All junctions identified by MAJIQ & All novel junctions identified by MAJIQ \\
\midrule
TP & TP-known: LR known + MAJIQ & TP-novel: LR novel + MAJIQ novel \\
FP & FP-known: LR known, no MAJIQ & FP-novel: LR novel, no MAJIQ \\
TN & \textit{Not possible (unknown expression)} & \textit{Not possible by MAJIQ} \\
FN & FN-known: No LR, Yes MAJIQ & FN-novel: No LR, Yes MAJIQ novel \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
\vspace{0.3cm}

Note that \textbf{TN cannot be computed} with MAJIQ-L because we do not know if a gene is expressed.

\textbf{False Discovery Rate (FDR)} is computed as: FDR = $100 \times (N - TP) / N$, where N is the number of observed transcripts (TUSCO evaluation) or LR junctions (MAJIQ-L). For TUSCO evaluation, N = TP + PTP + FP (observed transcripts); for MAJIQ-L, N = total\_LR\_junctions and TP includes both known and novel.

\subsection*{Comparison of Benchmarking Insights}

We compared TUSCO evaluation with MAJIQ-L, a junction-level benchmarking approach using matched short-read data as a proxy reference (Supplementary Table~2). Among LRGASP pipelines, cDNA-PacBio consistently ranked best under both frameworks, while cDNA-ONT and dRNA-ONT rankings diverged (Supplementary Fig.~4). This divergence reflects complementary quality dimensions: TUSCO evaluation is sensitive to RNA integrity (detecting partial transcripts), whereas MAJIQ-L reflects sequencing depth across all expressed junctions. For example, dRNA-ONT achieves high TUSCO evaluation sensitivity (75.6\%) despite only 760K reads, but shows a high MAJIQ-L false-negative rate (73.5\%) due to limited depth. Conversely, cDNA-ONT has 16.6M reads and better MAJIQ-L junction sensitivity (37.0\%) but lower TUSCO evaluation sensitivity (59.5\%) due to degraded RNA. These results demonstrate that TUSCO evaluation and MAJIQ-L provide complementary assessments of long-read transcriptome reconstruction quality. The two methods differ fundamentally in their approach. MAJIQ-L evaluates junction-level metrics and can assess novel junctions not present in the annotation, while TUSCO evaluation evaluates transcript-level metrics including full transcript structure and is limited to consistently expressed genes in the TUSCO gene set. A key advantage of TUSCO evaluation is its ability to evaluate TSS and TTS accuracy through the PTP (partially true positive) category, which MAJIQ-L cannot assess. Furthermore, TUSCO evaluation provides a true ground truth based on the reference annotation, whereas MAJIQ-L uses short-read data as an approximation of the truth.

\subsection*{Conclusion}

TUSCO evaluation and MAJIQ-L capture different aspects of long-read transcriptomics performance. TUSCO evaluation provides a true annotation-based ground truth rather than relying on short-read approximations, can evaluate both PTP and FN at the transcript level, requires no additional sequencing costs (no matched short-read data needed), and uniquely captures TSS and TTS accuracy through the PTP category. The revised version of the manuscript includes these analyses and conclusions in Section 2.4 (Results, Line 258), Section 3 (Discussion, Line 406), Section 4.5 (Methods, Line 656), Supplementary Figure 4, and Supplementary Table 2.

\subsection{Comment 8: ``Biologically plausible'' definition}

\begin{reviewercomment}
The TUSCO-novel claim of ``biologically plausible isoforms'' (section 2.5) is interesting, but the validity of ``biologically plausible'' is unclear. Were the made-up splice sites chosen to e.g. preserve splice motifs / enhancers, etc? What about exon length and GC content bias?
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
In the current \tuscoNovel{} simulator implementation, the artificial isoforms are designed to be \emph{structurally plausible} in the limited sense of satisfying basic splice-junction sequence constraints. For multi-exon transcripts, each annotated intron is modified by shifting the donor and acceptor coordinates \emph{within the original intron bounds} while searching the gene's genomic sequence for canonical dinucleotides: \texttt{GT} at the donor and \texttt{AG} at the acceptor on the plus strand, and the corresponding reference-strand motifs \texttt{AC} (donor) and \texttt{CT} (acceptor) on the minus strand. The search is directional (donor downstream / acceptor upstream on the plus strand) with a default window of 100~bp, falling back to scanning the full intron if needed, and it enforces a minimum shift of 5~bp when a suitable motif can be found. For single-exon transcripts, the simulator introduces one synthetic intron by selecting two positions separated by at least 50~bp and then adjusting each boundary to a nearby canonical motif within 20~bp when possible.

That said, several aspects of \emph{biological} plausibility are not modeled or guaranteed. Splice enhancer/silencer sequences are not preserved, branch point / polypyrimidine tract features are not verified, and GC content or exon-length distributions are not matched. The goal is not to generate transcripts that would necessarily be spliced in vivo, but to create misleading annotations that are superficially consistent with simple splice-site rules and could plausibly appear in a reference annotation. This tests whether reconstruction tools primarily follow the supplied annotation or instead use read evidence to recover the true splice pattern.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Changes in Manuscript:} We have revised the term to ``structurally plausible'' rather than ``biologically plausible'' and clarified in the Methods (Section 4.3, lines 634--638) that artificial splice sites preserve canonical motifs but do not guarantee functional splicing.

\subsection{Comment 12: Per-read coverage definition}

\begin{reviewercomment}
It is not clear why per-read coverage is defined and used as in Section 2.5.
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
In Section~2.5, we investigate why some \tuscoGeneSet{} loci are classified as TP (full match, including boundaries) while others are PTP (no novel junction chain, but shifted TSS/TTS). For Iso-Seq data, many PTPs are expected from 5'/3' truncated molecules resulting from RNA degradation or incomplete capture: reads can support the correct junction chain but fail to span the true transcript ends, yielding boundary-shifted models unless transcript ends are refined.

To quantify this end-completeness, we define per-read coverage as $f = L_{\mathrm{read}}/L_{\mathrm{exonic}}$, where $L_{\mathrm{exonic}}$ is the total exonic length of the true reference isoform and $L_{\mathrm{read}}$ is the observed Iso-Seq read length (PacBio cDNA; FLNC). With this definition, $f \approx 1$ indicates a near full-length molecule, $f \ll 1$ indicates truncation, and $f$ can exceed 1 when a read is longer than the reference isoform (e.g., extra UTR/poly(A) sequence). Because transcript models are constructed directly from the supporting reads, truncated reads lead to incomplete transcript models with shifted TSS/TTS boundaries---precisely the PTP outcome. Consistent with this, TP reads show high coverage (median 98.3\% in human), whereas PTP reads show substantially lower coverage (median 31.7\% in human), confirming that PTP arises from truncated input molecules rather than algorithmic errors.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Changes in Manuscript:} The per-read coverage metric is defined in Section 2.5 (Results, lines 312--319):
\begin{quote}
We quantified per-read coverage $f = \frac{L_{\mathrm{read}}}{L_{\mathrm{exonic}}}$ for each read supporting TP or PTP calls in the \tuscoGeneSet{}, where $L_{\mathrm{read}}$ is the observed Iso-Seq read length (PacBio cDNA; FLNC) and $L_{\mathrm{exonic}}$ is the total exonic length of the true reference isoform. Thus, $f \approx 1$ indicates near full-length support, whereas low $f$ indicates 5'/3' truncation expected under RNA degradation or incomplete capture (and $f$ can exceed 1 when a read is longer than the reference isoform).
\end{quote}
\end{responseboxnew}

\subsection{Comment 2: Section 2.5 - TUSCO-novel evaluation}

\begin{reviewercomment}
The capacity of reconstruction tools to detect unannotated isoforms is assessed with used of introduced TUSCO-novel reference set. The authors note that tools which heavily rely on annotation fail to recover new isoforms as defined in TUSCO-novel. That is true, but what was not highlighted or noted is the fact that preferred by authors Iso-Seq+SQANTI3 is not way better. It has marginal drop of performance between ``reference annotation'' and TUSCO-novel due to the fact that is dealing with ``reference annotation'' noticeably worse than other approaches. Thus ability of TUSCO-novel to evaluate novel isoforms discovery is not that remarkable I would say.
\end{reviewercomment}

\begin{responseboxnew}
The reviewer makes an interesting observation. Summarizing Fig.~4b by F1 score, Bambu achieves near-perfect performance under reference annotation (F1: 99.5\%, range 97.9--100.0 in human), while Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 ML scores lower (F1: 54.5\%). However, under TUSCO-novel---where the reference annotation is deliberately incorrect---Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 ML's F1 remains stable (57.1\%), while Bambu's F1 drops dramatically to 37.3\% (range 30.5--53.1) (Supplementary Table~3). This demonstrates that Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 ML's moderate but stable performance is largely independent of annotation quality.

The key insight from TUSCO-novel is not absolute performance but annotation dependence. Bambu and StringTie2 are strongly reference-guided and therefore highly dependent on reference annotation; in settings where gene-level annotation is largely complete but isoform-level annotation is missing or incorrect, this reliance can limit their ability to detect genuinely novel isoforms. Consistent with this, when the annotation at loci in the TUSCO gene set is replaced by an incorrect splice pattern, Bambu and StringTie2 fail substantially to recover the true isoforms despite read support. Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 ML, by contrast, performs consistently regardless of annotation status. The lower baseline for Iso-Seq arises because the pipeline lacks TSS/TTS refinement against the reference, resulting in more PTP calls even when the junction chain is correct. This reduces TP rates but makes the pipeline inherently reference-agnostic.

The practical implication of this observation is that in scenarios where reference annotations may be incomplete or misleading (novel tissues, disease states, non-model organisms), a pipeline with lower but \textit{stable} performance may be more desirable than one that performs well only when guided by complete annotations but degrades sharply otherwise. In such settings, maintaining very high RNA quality becomes especially important to minimize the risk of PTP.

\vspace{0.3cm}
\textbf{Changes in Manuscript:} We have revised Section 2.5 (lines 292--303) to explicitly acknowledge this trade-off:

\begin{quote}
``By contrast, Iso-Seq + SQANTI3 ML ($n=1$ per species) showed stable F1 regardless of annotation (human: 54.5\% to 57.1\%; mouse: 51.0\% to 48.9\%) and reported 0 FP under TUSCO-novel.''
\end{quote}
\end{responseboxnew}

Regarding Supplementary Table~4, the per-sample FLNC read counts provide the depth context needed to interpret these replicate effects and to generalize them: they allow readers to compare their expected per-sample yield to ours and, under a fixed sequencing budget, to evaluate the trade-off between sequencing deeper versus adding replicates.
